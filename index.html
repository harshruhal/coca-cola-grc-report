1. Executive Summary

This GRC (Governance, Risk, and Compliance) Cybersecurity Audit Report presents a comprehensive independent assessment of The Coca-Cola Company's cybersecurity posture. The audit was conducted using publicly available information including SEC Form 10-K filings (2023–2025), regulatory records, breach disclosures, cybersecurity research databases, and news sources.

The Coca-Cola Company is one of the world's largest consumer goods corporations, operating in over 200 countries with more than 700,000 employees. The company processes massive volumes of consumer, employee, financial, and supply chain data — making robust cybersecurity governance a legal and business necessity.

Key Findings

  • Coca-Cola has a well-structured cybersecurity governance model with a CISO (25+ years experience) reporting to the CIO and quarterly Audit Committee briefings.
  • The company has experienced at least 5 cybersecurity incidents between 2014 and 2025, demonstrating persistent threat exposure.
  • Critical compliance gap: inadequate breach notification. The 2025 Everest/Gehenna incidents (23M records) were not publicly confirmed — potential SEC and GDPR violations.
  • Third-party bottler supply chain is the #1 systemic risk — CCEP and regional distributors are repeat breach vectors.

2.2 Why Coca-Cola is a Critical GRC Audit Target

  • Global Scale: Operations in 200+ countries means exposure to dozens of national data protection laws simultaneously.
  • Consumer Data Volume: Mobile apps, loyalty programs, e-commerce platforms, and vending IoT devices collect consumer data at massive scale.
  • Third-Party Ecosystem: Hundreds of independent bottlers create significant supply chain risk that Coca-Cola cannot fully control.
  • Public Listing: NYSE-listed company subject to SEC cybersecurity disclosure rules (December 2023), SOX requirements, and shareholder scrutiny.
  • Brand Value: Consistently ranked among the world's most valuable brands — a major breach causes irreparable reputational damage.

5.2 SOX Analysis

  • As a NYSE-listed company, Coca-Cola complies with SOX Sections 302 and 404.
  • CEO James Quincey and CFO personally certify financial reports and disclosure controls each quarter.
  • Ernst & Young (external auditor) assesses internal controls over financial reporting annually.
  • Audit Finding: SOX COMPLIANT — No material weaknesses identified in public filings.

5.3 GDPR Analysis

  • Coca-Cola has a dedicated EU Privacy Counsel position and integrates 'privacy by design' into business processes.
  • COMPLIANCE GAP: The 2025 Everest/Gehenna breach affecting European partners (CCEP) was NOT publicly confirmed — potential GDPR Article 33 violation (72-hour notification requirement).
  • Audit Finding: PARTIAL COMPLIANCE — Privacy program exists but breach notification obligations appear unmet.

5.4 SEC Cybersecurity Disclosure Rules (December 2023)

  • Public companies must disclose material cybersecurity incidents within 4 business days via SEC Form 8-K.
  • Annual disclosure of cybersecurity governance, strategy, and management expertise is required in 10-K.
  • Coca-Cola complies with the annual governance disclosure (detailed cybersecurity sections in 10-K filings).
  • COMPLIANCE GAP: The 2025 incident involving 23 million records has not been publicly confirmed. If material, an 8-K filing is required. Audit Finding: AT RISK.

7. Conclusion

This GRC Cybersecurity Audit of The Coca-Cola Company reveals an organization with a fundamentally sound governance architecture that is nonetheless challenged by persistent execution gaps — particularly around third-party security, incident detection, and breach notification.

Coca-Cola's strongest area is Governance: a highly qualified CISO, a structured Cybersecurity Oversight Council, and board-level Audit Committee oversight places the company above average for its industry. SEC-mandated annual disclosure of cybersecurity governance further demonstrates institutional commitment.

However, the Risk Management record reveals recurring vulnerabilities. Five distinct cybersecurity incidents over 11 years (2014–2025) — spanning insider threats, phishing, ransomware, and large-scale CRM exfiltration — paint a picture of reactive rather than proactive security management. The 2025 Everest and Gehenna incidents, collectively involving 23 million records, represent the most serious test of Coca-Cola's security posture to date.

Compliance gaps are most visible in breach notification. The failure to publicly confirm the 2025 incidents creates exposure under both SEC cybersecurity disclosure rules and GDPR Article 33. The absence of publicly confirmed ISO 27001 certification for the parent entity is a notable gap for a company of this scale and global reach.

The primary systemic vulnerability is the franchised bottling model. Until minimum security standards are enforced across the entire bottling ecosystem, Coca-Cola's overall risk exposure will remain elevated regardless of how strong its internal governance becomes.
